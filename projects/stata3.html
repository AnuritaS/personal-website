Part 1: Sampling noise in a fixed population

Generated a fixed population of 10,000 individuals with a true Y–X relationship (seed-fixed for reproducibility), then ran 500 regression simulations at four sample sizes to quantify how estimation precision changes as we draw larger subsamples from a bounded population.

Key finding: At N=10, beta estimates vary wildly across simulations despite being unbiased on average. At N=10,000 (the full population), all sampling variance disappears entirely — this is the finite-population ceiling.

Sample size (N) Mean β̂ Avg. SEM 95% CI width % significant
10 ≈ true β High Very wide ~30–40%
100 ≈ true β Moderate Wide ~70–80%
1,000 ≈ true β Small Narrow ~98%+
10,000 = true β Near zero Minimal 100%

Part 2: Sampling noise in an infinite superpopulation

Extended the simulation to an infinite superpopulation DGP — generating fresh data each run — across 26 sample sizes: all 20 powers of 2 (4 → 524,288) plus N = 10, 100, 1,000, 10,000, 100,000, and 1,000,000.

Why superpopulation allows N = 1,000,000: In Part 1 the population is capped at 10,000. The superpopulation DGP draws from an infinite distribution each run — sample size is unbounded.

SEM difference: In the superpopulation, variance shrinks as 1/√N but never hits zero. At the same N, superpopulation SEM is slightly larger than fixed-population SEM, because the fixed population’s known finite structure removes a layer of uncertainty.

Part 3: Individual-level power calculations

Designed a DGP where Y ~ N(0,1), treatment effect = 0.1 SD (uniform 0.0–0.2 SD), 50/50 treatment-control split. Calculated minimum sample sizes for 80% power under three scenarios:

~3,142
Baseline N (80% power, 0.1 SD effect)
~3,697
After 15% attrition (+18% larger)
~4,190
30% treatment arm (unequal split)

Attrition: Losing 15% of participants symmetrically inflates required sample by ~18% — you must recruit enough to still have sufficient power after dropout.

Unequal arms: Shifting to 30/70 treatment-control requires a larger total N. The smaller arm is the binding constraint on precision. The 50/50 split is always the most efficient design for a fixed total N.

Part 4: Cluster RCT design — ICC = 0.3

Designed a school-based intervention where treatment is assigned at the school level — not student level. Treatment effect = 0.2 SD (uniform 0.15–0.25 SD), ICC/ρ ≈ 0.3.

Design effect at 15 students/school: DEFF = 1 + (15–1) × 0.3 = 5.2 — meaning you need roughly 5× as many observations as an individual-level RCT.

Power plateaus quickly: With 200 schools fixed, power reaches ~80% around 8–16 students/school. Beyond that, adding students yields near-zero gains due to high ICC.

Scenario Schools needed Total students
Full compliance (100% uptake) ~100 schools ~1,500
70% adoption (partial compliance) ~145 schools ~2,175

Partial compliance finding: When only 70% of assigned schools implement the intervention, the required school count rises by ~45%. Non-compliance is a design problem, not just a fidelity problem.
